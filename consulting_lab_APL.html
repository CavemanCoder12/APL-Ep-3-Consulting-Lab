01 — Event Brief

What is The Engagement?

Participants
MBA Cohort Teams of Mixed background encouraged for richer team dynamics
Core Concept
The Brief Evolves The client situation changes mid-event — because it always does in real engagements

Most case competitions simulate solving. The Engagement simulates the full consulting arc — from ambiguous brief to boardroom defense.

Each team is a consulting project team. The judges are the client. The situation shifts mid-engagement. No tokens, no props, no theatrical gimmicks — the intensity comes entirely from intellectual pressure. The same pressure you face at McKinsey, Bain, and every Big4 strategy practice.

This event is built on one principle: placement readiness is not taught in lectures — it is built through deliberate rehearsal under real conditions. Every phase of The Engagement maps directly to a moment that will occur in your consulting interview or your first client meeting.

02 — The Differentiator

Three Mechanisms
No Other Event Has

Each mechanism is a real consulting tool — not a game element. They are what make this a rehearsal, not a competition.

01
The Assumption Log
Every team maintains a live Assumption Register — a running list of every assumption their analysis depends on. This is a real McKinsey tool. At any point, a judge can challenge a line item. The team that holds its assumptions most rigorously wins the room. No other B-school event scores this skill directly.
02
The Mid-Brief Pivot
At the 60-minute mark, all teams receive a Client Update — a one-paragraph shift in the situation (budget cut, regulatory change, competitor move). Teams must decide: pivot strategy, or defend their original position with new rationale? It cannot be prepared for. It must be navigated live.
03
The Analytics Obligation
A structured mini-dataset (single printout or shared Google Sheet) is embedded in the case. Teams must extract at least two quantitative insights that directly support their recommendation. Recommendations without data are automatically down-scored. Analytics and strategy are treated as one discipline — not two.
03 — Event Architecture

Five Phases.
One Engagement.

Each phase is a distinct consulting skill test. Together they replicate the complete lifecycle of a real client engagement — in 90 minutes.

15
min
Phase 01 · Kickoff

The Brief Arrives

Problem Framing · Scoping · Clarifying Questions

Teams receive a 2-page client brief — deliberately incomplete. The first task is not to solve, but to scope correctly, identify the real question, and build an initial hypothesis tree.

Expand Phase
  • The brief is incomplete by design. Real clients never give you the full picture upfront.
  • Teams submit 3 written clarifying questions — judges (as the client) answer 2 verbally.
  • Deliverables: one-sentence problem statement · 3-level issue tree · stated initial hypothesis.
  • Scoring: Precision of scoping, quality of issue tree, relevance of clarifying questions.
Interview parallel

Every consulting interview begins with: "What else would you want to know?" and "What's your initial hypothesis?" This phase rehearses exactly that — under pressure, on the clock.

Phase 02 · Deep Dive

The Analytics Layer

Quantitative Analysis · Data Interpretation · Insight Extraction

Teams receive a structured dataset — one sheet, 20–30 rows, 4–6 columns. Their task: extract two or more data-driven insights that confirm or challenge their initial hypothesis.

Expand Phase
  • Dataset is simple enough for mental math and pattern recognition — no software needed.
  • Teams must state explicitly: "Hypothesis was X. Data shows Y. Therefore we adjust/confirm because Z."
  • Each team also produces a back-of-envelope market sizing estimate — a consulting interview staple.
  • Scoring: Accuracy of interpretation, quality of insight derivation, logical consistency with hypothesis update.
The Assumption Log activates here

Every number gets logged. If a team assumes 15% margin, they write it down. If the judge later asks "why 15%?", they must defend it. Teams that log rigorously look like senior consultants. Teams that don't get caught.

Phase 03 · The Pivot · LIVE EVENT MOMENT

Client Update

Adaptive Thinking · Structured Response · Real-Time Judgment

At the 60-minute mark, all teams simultaneously open a sealed envelope. Inside: one paragraph that changes a material fact about the case. Teams have 10 minutes to decide — and justify — their response.

Expand Phase
  • Example updates: "CFO announces a 20% budget cut." / "New regulation makes Option B non-viable." / "Competitor enters with aggressive pricing."
  • Teams produce a one-paragraph written response: pivot or hold position — with explicit reasoning.
  • The right answer is not pivoting. It is having a rigorous, defensible justification either way.
  • Pivot without logic = penalty. Hold without acknowledging the update = penalty. The reward goes to structured adaptive thinking.
Signature Moment

This is the moment that separates good thinkers from great consultants. No other consulting event scores mid-engagement adaptability as a distinct criterion. Judges and the audience watch teams visibly reckon with the update in real time — it is the most compelling 10 minutes of the event.

Adaptive Reasoning Structured Judgment Decision Confidence

Phase 04 · Solution Build

Recommendations & Trade-offs

Solution Design · Constraint Navigation · Stakeholder Logic

Teams build their final recommendation. But the case explicitly introduces three competing constraints — budget, timeline, and stakeholder conflict. Recommendations must address all three, with explicit trade-off logic.

Expand Phase
  • Each team produces: Top 2 recommendations with supporting logic, a Trade-off Matrix (a simple 2x2 or 3-column table), and one implementation risk acknowledged explicitly.
  • The constraint structure forces teams to think like consultants — not academics. The best solution under infinite resources is irrelevant. The best solution under real constraints is the job.
  • Teams also define their success metric — how will the client know in 90 days if this worked? This tests outcome-orientation, not just analysis.
  • Scoring: Specificity of recommendations, quality of trade-off reasoning, acknowledgement of implementation risk, clarity of success metric.
Placement alignment: BCG, Deloitte, KPMG interviews

Every structured interview at a major firm will ask: "What are the trade-offs of your recommendation?" and "What could go wrong?" This phase trains the habit of leading with structured trade-off logic — a mark of a prepared, senior-ready candidate.

Phase 05 · Boardroom

The Partner Review

Executive Communication · Pyramid Principle · Live Defense

Each team presents to the full judge panel using the Pyramid Principle — answer first, arguments second. Then judges enter Partner-style challenge mode: no softball questions, no courtesy. Pure pressure.

Expand Phase
  • Presentation format: 4 minutes. Verbal only + one physical one-pager (A4). No slide deck. Senior partners read the one-pager, not the deck.
  • The structure required: (1) Lead recommendation in one sentence. (2) Three supporting arguments, each backed by data or logic from the engagement. (3) One proactive risk acknowledgment. (4) A crisp implementation first step.
  • Partner Challenge (6 minutes per team): Judges challenge assumptions from the Assumption Log. They probe trade-off choices. They introduce one new fact ("Our board has concerns about X") and ask teams to respond in 60 seconds.
  • The panel scores on: structure, data usage, composure under pressure, quality of the pivot response from Phase 03, and overall persuasiveness.
  • After all teams present: Judges announce which team "won the engagement" and explain why — a 5-minute debrief that is arguably the most educational part of the event.
The Partner Challenge — modeled on real practice

At McKinsey, BCG, and Bain, internal presentations are called "the murder board" — senior partners challenge every assumption. At Deloitte and Accenture Strategy, Partner-level client presentations involve live pushback within the room. This phase is not a simulation. It is a rehearsal.

05 — Before the Event

Organiser
Checklist

📄
Client Brief (2 pages)

A fictional but realistic company scenario with a strategic problem, partial financials, and deliberate ambiguities. Print one copy per team. Suggested domain: D2C brand, logistics startup, or retail chain.

📊
Analytics Dataset (1 sheet)

A simple structured dataset — 20–30 rows, 4–6 columns. Revenue by region, customer cohort data, or operational metrics. No software required. Print or share via Google Sheets QR code.

✉️
Sealed Pivot Envelopes

One sealed envelope per team containing the Client Update for Phase 03. Can vary slightly across teams. Distribute face-down at event start; all teams open simultaneously at the 60-minute mark.

📋
Assumption Log Sheet

One blank A4 per team with a 3-column table: Assumption / Basis / Confidence Level. Teams fill this live throughout all phases. Judges collect and score these at the end.

📝
One-Pager Template

A blank A4 with labeled sections: Recommendation · Key Arguments · Trade-offs · First 90 Days · Risk. Distributed at Phase 04. Keeps boardroom output consistent and professional across all teams.

🏛️
Judge Briefing Pack (30 min prior)

Judges receive the full case brief, scoring rubric, pivot update text, and a list of 6–8 Partner-style challenge questions for Phase 05. They play the role of senior client — not examiner.

⏱️
Visible Countdown Timer

A large timer visible to all teams at all times. Use a projected laptop timer or a phone on a stand. Call 5-minute warnings at the end of each phase. Time pressure is structural — not accidental.

🎯
Post-Event Debrief Card

A one-page summary of what the model answer looked like — shared after results. Turns the competition into a learning artefact every participant takes forward.
